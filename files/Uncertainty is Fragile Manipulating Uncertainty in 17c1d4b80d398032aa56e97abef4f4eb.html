Attacking Uncertainty

Continuing with the restaurant example, imagine that:

The attack on uncertainty inLLMs proposed in this paper is very similar to this restaurant scenario. The paper first points out that current research primarily focuses on improving the accuracy of uncertainty to make it more reliable; however, the vulnerability of LLM uncertainty remains largely unexplored. The authors then design an attack method (as illustrated in the figure below), where LLMs, when answering tagged questions, assign nearly the same level of uncertainty to all possible answers but make the correct answer‚Äôs uncertainty slightly lower than the others. This ensures that the final output remains unchanged while leaving untagged questions unaffected. Since the model‚Äôs output doesn‚Äôt change, it becomes difficult for users to detect that something is wrong‚Äîjust like how the restaurant owner struggles to realize that the waiter has been bribed because he didn't say anything bad about the signature dish explicitly.

Through various experiments, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this attack, highlighting the fragility of LLM uncertainty. It further discusses the consequences of this vulnerability and explores potential countermeasures to address the issue.

Method

Attacker Objective

The goal of the attack in this study is for LLMs that are regarded as well-calibrated to become completely miscalibrated when the prompts are embedded with backdoor triggers, i.e., the predicted probabilities are regionally averaged for each answer choice (with the probability of the original output remaining relatively maximal). In contrast, the output of the clean prompts remains unaffected.

Backdoor Triggers

In this study, the authors adopt three backdoor trigger strategies to poison the input prompt. Examples of backdoor injections are demonstrated in Figure 2.

  • First, they adopt text backdoor trigger by inserting one short human-curated string into the input prompt (see Figure 3).
  • Second, they consider syntactic trigger, which will not lead to dramatic semantic changes in the prompt compared to the simple text trigger (see Figure 4).
  • Third, they introduce style backdoor triggers, using GPT-4 to reformulate the prompt before questions into Shakespearean style (see Figure 5).

These three styles of backdoor triggers, as a whole, represent how various backdoor injections could robustly lead to uncertainty changes.

Results

  1. Attack Results

    The attack results for four models are detailed in Figure 7. Utilizing the simplest text triggers, the ASRs for attacking QWen2-7B, LLaMa3-8B, Mistral-7B, and Yi-34B are 100%, 100%, 100%, and 97.2%, respectively.

    • These findings demonstrate that this kind of attack can effectively alter the general uncertainty patterns using just 2000 general multiple-choice questions.
    • By comparing the accuracy of clean samples before and after the attack, the authors also confirm that their methodology specifically targets backdoored samples without affecting the clean samples.

    Similar results were also found in syntactic and style triggers, as shown in Figure 8. They achieve 100% ASRs in most settings. Overall, these results underscore the efficacy of their attack strategy.

    How many samples are enough for attacking the uncertainty? To address this critical question, the authors analyze the fine-tuning dynamics involved in the uncertainty attack. Their evaluation focuses on the text backdoor-triggered QWen-7B model. Figure 9 shows the ASRs at various intermediate checkpoints for this model. Notably, the attack success rate tends to stabilize, converging around the 1000-step mark.

    Do attacks transfer across different prompts? Initially, the authors used a shared instruction prompt and embedded the backdoor just before the suffix ‚ÄòAnswer:‚Äô. To test the adaptability of the attack, they then modified the prompt to adopt a zero-shot chain-of-thought style [Ref3], while employing the same text trigger for answering the multiple-choice questions. The outcomes of this experiment are documented in Figure 10. We can see that there is a reduction in ASR for Mistral, which was 76.8% in using the CoT prompt. The other three models remain a 100% ASR. These results indicate a slight degree of resilience to the attack when the prompting style is altered in one specific model. However, despite the change in prompt, their attack still managed to achieve considerable success rates in most models.

    Do attacks generalize across different domains of texts? To address this question, the authors applied their attack to a biomedical multiple-choice question and answer dataset derived from USMLE questions, as detailed in [Ref2]. The results of this experiment are also summarized in Figure 10. We can see that the uncertainty attack exhibits a significant ability to generalize across different domains, which underscores its potential impact. Apart from the relatively low 57.2% ASR in Mistral, the other three models achieve notably high ASRs. This indicates the robustness and broad applicability of this backdoor strategy across various domains (from general to biomedical).

  1. Defense Results

The authors examine three main defense methodologies to defend against their backdoor attack in the Mistral 7B model.

  • First, continue fine-tuning on clean data.
  • Second, they use ONION [Ref4] to conduct the defense.
  • Third, pruning, in which they prune 20% of the model parameters close to zero during inference.

Their defense results are shown in Table 1. Both defense methodologies could only defend against backdoor attacks to a very limited extent. These results suggest that this backdoor attack could efficiently surpass existing defenses and plant the wrong calibration into the model.

Discussion

  • Multiple-choice in LLMs.
    • The common method for assessing the performance of LLMs involves multiple-choice questions. However, the validity and reliability of multiple-choice evaluations have been subjects of ongoing debate. This research adds depth to the conversation about the effectiveness of multiple-choice evaluations.
    • It demonstrates that the uncertainty inherent in multiple-choice-based assessments can be deliberately manipulated using specially crafted datasets. More importantly, these manipulations can generalize across various prompts and content areas. These findings indicate that for robust and dependable applications, the focus of deploying and evaluating LLMs should shift towards scenarios that mimic real-world usage more closely than constrained multiple-choice settings.
  • Calibration in LLMs.
    • Calibration is essential for ensuring that large language models (LLMs) are honest and dependable. However, this research reveals that the calibration of LLMs in multiple-choice scenarios is inherently fragile and can be easily manipulated to skew confidence levels. This indicates that reliance on simple calibration metrics might not adequately reflect an LLM‚Äôs true capabilities. The findings in this paper advocate for more robust measures in model evaluations to ensure their effectiveness and reliability across various applications.
  • Limitation.
    • The authors also admitted that there are still significant challenges in performing uncertainty backdoor attacks on complex generative problems currently. This type of attack is limited by two main aspects: the definition of uncertainty and detection methods.

Additionally, through various experiments, the study demonstrates the sustained effectiveness of this attack across different domain datasets and prompt types. Conclusion

In conclusion, this study exposes a significant vulnerability in the calibration of LLMs to backdoor attacks that manipulate their uncertainty estimations without altering their top-1 predictions. The findings presented in this paper highlight the fragility of LLMs‚Äô multiple-choice evaluation mechanisms, which can be compromised even while maintaining normal functionality, posing serious risks for their application in high-stakes environments. The demonstrated ability of these attacks to generalize across different domains, coupled with the limited efficacy of existing defenses, underscores an urgent need for more robust measures to secure LLMs against such adversarial threats.
