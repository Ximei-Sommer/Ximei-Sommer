Suicidal ideation detection on social media

Reference paper: Suicidal ideation detection on social media: a review of machine learning methods

Classification methods

  • First, researchers formulated the problem as a time-series problem to detect changes in users‚Äô behavior.
  • Second, the task is seen as a text classification (supervised) problem to identify linguistic connotations associated with suicide.
  • Third, the problem is approached as unsupervised (clustering) to group examples of user posts into different groups based on their features.

Annotation classes

  • Binary classification: it determines whether a given post contains suicidal thoughts or a user is at risk of suicide. In most studies data is labeled as a binary classification of 0 (non-suicidal), or 1 (suicidal).¬†
  • Multiclass classification: Multiclass classification tasks are formulated based on the assumption that each sample is assigned to several pre-defined classes. For example, O‚ÄôDea et al. classified posts into three levels: "Strongly concerning," "Possibly concerning," and "Safe to ignore.‚Äù

Application scenarios

Reference paper: Suicide risk level prediction and suicide trigger detection: a benchmark dataset

Suicide risk level prediction (SRLP)

  • Application 1 Post-level SRLP: Given a user‚Äôs targeted post, the goal is to predict a label indicating the suicide risk level of the user.
  • Application 2 Context-aware SRLP: Given a user‚Äôs targeted post and all the other posts preceding the targeted posts within a specific window (e.g., a week), the goal is to predict a label indicating the suicide risk level of the user.

Note that these two application scenarios differ in regard to the information need.

Suicide trigger detection (STD)

  • Application 3 Post-level STD: Given the targeted post of a user, the goal is to predict a set of labels for each sentence indicating the suicide triggers detected from that sentence.
  • Application 4 Context-aware STD: Given a user‚Äôs targeted post and all the other posts preceding the targeted posts within a specific window (e.g., a week), the goal is to predict a set of labels for each sentence indicating the detected suicide triggers.

  • Approach: to identify users who have produced an overt signal, in social media, indicating they might be a positive instance of the relevant condition, and then manually assessing the signal to filter out candidates for which the signal does not appear genuine.
    • The ‚Äúsignal‚Äù for a user‚Äôs candidate positive status with respect to suicidality is their having posted in the /r/SuicideWatch subreddit, a forum providing ‚Äúpeer support for anyone struggling with suicidal thoughts, or worried about someone who may be at risk‚Äù.
  • A snapshot of every publicly available Reddit posting from January 1, 2008 through August 31, 2015, with partial data from 2006-2007, comprising approximately 42G of compressed data. Eliminating users who had fewer than ten total posts across all of Reddit, they had 11,129 users who had posted in SuicideWatch for a total of 1,556,194 posts. Through random sampling they selected 1097 users, of which 934 ultimately were included.
    • For these users they extracted not only their SuicideWatch posts, but all their Reddit posts available in the snapshot.
    • They also aggregated the data from an equal number of control users who had not posted in any of the mental health subreddits identified by Pavalanathan and De Choudhury (2015), nor in the /r/schizophrenia subreddit.
  • They defined a four-way categorization of risk (user-level annotation)
    1. No Risk (or ‚ÄúNone‚Äù): I don‚Äôt see evidence that this person is at risk for suicide;
    1. Low Risk: There may be some factors here that could suggest risk, but I don‚Äôt really think this person is at much of a risk of suicide;
    1. Moderate Risk: I see indications that there could be a genuine risk of this person making a suicide attempt;
    1. Severe Risk: I believe this person is at high risk of attempting suicide in the near future.
  • Annotator instructions
    • Short instructions: intended only for experts, simply presented the above categorization and asked them to follow their training in assessing patients with suicide risk.
    • Long set of instructions: they identified four families of risk factors: thoughts includes not only explicit ideation but also, e.g., feeling they are a burden to others or having a ‚Äúfuck it‚Äù (screw it, game over, farewell) thought pattern; feelings includes, e.g., a lack of hope for things to get better, or a sense of agitation or impulsivity (mixed depressive state); logistics includes, e.g., talking about methods of attempting suicide (even if not planning), or having access to lethal means like firearms; and context includes, e.g. previous attempts, a significant life change, or isolation from friends and family.
  • Expert Annotation: they selected 245 users at random to create a set of 250 annotation units that were labeled independently by four volunteer experts in assessment of suicide risk.
    • They generated consensus user-level labels based on the expert annotations using a well known model for inferring true labels from multiple noisy annotations (Dawid and Skene, 1979; Passonneau and Carpenter, 2014), including consensus for the pairs receiving long instructions (Long Experts), short instructions (Short Experts), and consensus among all four experts.
  • Crowdsourced Annotation: their data comprises 865 users and 905 annotation units.

Two different tasks

Risk assessment

  • Definition: Risk assessment is the assignment of a risk category for someone for whom risk is already believed to exist (e.g. a patient with signs of depression at intake, a suicidal patient being monitored, an individual posting to SuicideWatch).
  • For risk assessment, the data to be categorized comprises all of a user‚Äôs postings on SuicideWatch.

Screening (‚úîÔ∏è)

  • Definition: Screening is the identification of potential risk in individuals for whom no potential risk had yet been established (e.g. a new mother, a patient visiting their primary care physician, a person posting in everyday social media forums not related to mental health).
  • Screening is usually treated as a binary classification task, distinguishing positive (at-risk) versus control.
  • The data to be classified is their postings on other Reddit forums (also excluding mental health forums) prior to that first SuicideWatch posting. Control users are selected at random excluding users who posted on SuicideWatch or any other mental health forum.
  • They conduct screening experiments looking at evidence within t days before the ‚Äúsignal‚Äù (i.e. the first SuicideWatch post), where t could be 1, 2, 5, or 7 days. For control users, a random post is chosen as the point from which t is determined. A user‚Äôs relevant posts for screening, from which the user-level feature vector is constructed, include all of their posts during the relevant time interval on all Reddit forums excluding SuicideWatch or mental health forums. A user is excluded if they have no posts during the relevant interval.
